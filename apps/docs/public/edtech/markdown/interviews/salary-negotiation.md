Master salary negotiation for developer roles. Learn to research compensation, negotiate confidently, evaluate offers, and maximize your total package.

Salary negotiation is a crucial skill that can increase your lifetime earnings by hundreds of thousands of dollars. Many developers—especially from cultures that discourage negotiation—leave significant money on the table by not negotiating. This guide provides practical strategies to negotiate effectively and confidently.

## Why Negotiation Matters

### The Math

**Lifetime Impact**:
```
Scenario A: Accept first offer
Starting salary: $100,000
Annual raises: 3%
After 10 years: $134,392

Scenario B: Negotiate 10% higher
Starting salary: $110,000
Annual raises: 3%
After 10 years: $147,831

Difference: $13,439 per year in year 10
Total difference over 10 years: ~$138,000

One conversation → $138,000

And this doesn't include:
- Bonuses (often percentage of base)
- 401k matching (percentage of base)
- Stock grants (sometimes tied to level)
```

**Compounding Effects**:
```
Higher starting salary leads to:
✓ Bigger raises (% of higher base)
✓ Larger bonuses (% of higher base)
✓ Better offers at next job (based on current salary)
✓ Higher lifetime Social Security benefits
✓ More retirement savings

Negotiating is one of the highest-ROI activities 
you'll ever do (thousands of dollars per hour).
```

### Why Companies Expect Negotiation

**The Game**:
```
Company perspective:
- Budget has flexibility
- First offer rarely their maximum
- Expect candidates to negotiate
- Respect candidates who negotiate
- May question commitment if you don't

Not negotiating signals:
- You don't know your value
- You're desperate for the job
- You won't advocate for yourself
- You might be easier to underpay

Negotiating shows:
- You know your worth
- You're confident
- You'll advocate for yourself
- You understand business
```

## When to Negotiate

### The Timeline

**Don't Discuss Salary Too Early**:
```
Wrong time (first conversation):
Recruiter: "What's your current salary?"
You: "$80,000"
[Now they anchor low]

Right time (after they want you):
Recruiter: "We'd like to make you an offer"
You: [NOW you negotiate]
```

**The Process**:
```
Stage 1: Initial Contact
- Recruiter reaches out
- ❌ Don't discuss salary yet
- Deflect: "I'm flexible on compensation and 
  more interested in the right opportunity"

Stage 2: Interviews
- Focus on showing your value
- ❌ Don't bring up salary
- If asked: "I'm focusing on finding the right 
  fit right now"

Stage 3: They Want You
- They say they want to move forward
- ❌ Still not quite time
- Response: "I'm excited too! What's the next step?"

Stage 4: Offer Made
- They present specific numbers
- ✓ NOW negotiate
- You have maximum leverage
```

### Avoiding Early Salary Questions

**"What's your current salary?"**:
```
Why they ask:
- Anchor your expectations low
- Determine your "market value"
- Save money on offer

How to deflect:

Option 1 (Redirect):
"I'm not comfortable sharing that, but I'm happy 
to discuss what I'm looking for if we determine 
we're a mutual fit."

Option 2 (Turn it around):
"I'd prefer to focus on the value I can bring to 
this role. Can you share the budget range for 
this position?"

Option 3 (In some US states, it's illegal to ask):
"I'm in [state], where that question isn't asked 
per salary history law. Happy to discuss my expectations 
for this role specifically."

Never:
❌ Lie about current salary (can be fired later)
❌ Feel pressured to answer
❌ Anchor yourself low
```

**"What are your salary expectations?"**:
```
How to deflect (early stages):

"I'm still learning about the role and want to make 
sure it's a good fit before discussing compensation. 
What's the range for this position?"

"I don't have a specific number in mind yet. I'm 
more focused on finding the right opportunity. What's 
typical for someone with my background in this role?"

"I'm sure if we decide to move forward, we can find 
a number that works for both of us. What range did 
you have in mind?"

Key: Make them give a number first (or at least a range)
```

## Research and Preparation

### Understanding Market Value

**Salary Research Tools**:
```
Websites:
- levels.fyi (tech companies, very accurate)
- Glassdoor (salary reports)
- Payscale (general market data)
- Blind (anonymous professional network)
- LinkedIn Salary
- H1B salary database (US positions)

For each, check:
- Your specific role
- Your location
- Your experience level
- Company size
- Total compensation (not just base)
```

**Factors Affecting Compensation**:
```
Location:
- San Francisco/NYC: Higher
- Austin/Seattle: High
- Smaller cities: Lower
- Remote: Often location-adjusted

Company Type:
- FAANG: $150k-$500k+ total comp
- Unicorn startup: $120k-$400k
- Series A-C startup: $90k-$200k
- Non-tech company: $70k-$150k

Experience:
- New grad: $80k-$150k
- Mid-level (3-5 years): $120k-$250k
- Senior (5-8 years): $150k-$350k
- Staff+ (8+ years): $200k-$500k+

Skills:
- Common stack: Average market rate
- In-demand skills: 10-30% premium
- Rare expertise: 30-50% premium
```

**Your Personal Baseline**:
```
Calculate your numbers:

Current total compensation:
+ Base salary: $______
+ Bonus (actual, not target): $______
+ Stock/equity (annualized): $______
+ Other benefits (value): $______
= Total: $______

Minimum acceptable offer (walk-away point):
$______ (should be > current comp)

Target offer (ambitious but realistic):
$______ (based on market research)

Stretch goal (best case):
$______ (high end of market range)

Know these numbers before negotiating!
```

### Understanding Total Compensation

**Components**:
```
1. Base Salary:
   - Guaranteed cash
   - Paid bi-weekly or monthly
   - Most stable component

2. Signing Bonus:
   - One-time cash payment
   - Usually paid in first paycheck
   - Often has 1-year clawback if you leave

3. Annual Bonus:
   - Performance-based cash
   - Target % of base (e.g., 10-20%)
   - Actual may be higher or lower

4. Equity/Stock:
   - RSUs (Restricted Stock Units): 
     * Actual company stock
     * Vests over time (typically 4 years)
     * Taxed as income when vested
     * Liquid if public company
   
   - Stock Options:
     * Right to buy stock at set price
     * Only valuable if company value increases
     * Illiquid until IPO/acquisition
     * May expire if you leave

5. Benefits:
   - Health insurance
   - 401k match
   - Paid time off
   - Learning budget
   - Remote work flexibility
```

**Calculating True Value**:
```
Example offer:

Base: $130,000
Bonus: 15% target = $19,500
RSUs: $100,000 over 4 years = $25,000/year
Signing bonus: $20,000 (one-time)

Total comp (year 1):
$130,000 + $19,500 + $25,000 + $20,000 = $194,500

Total comp (year 2-4):
$130,000 + $19,500 + $25,000 = $174,500

Average annual (4 years): $179,500

Compare this to other offers, not just base salary!
```

## Negotiation Strategies

### Getting the First Offer

**When They Ask Your Number**:
```
If you MUST give a number:

Strategy 1: Range based on research
"Based on my research for someone with my experience 
in this market, I'd expect $X to $Y total compensation."

[Where X = your target, Y = your stretch goal]

Strategy 2: Turn it around
"I'm flexible and mainly looking for the right opportunity. 
What range do you have budgeted for this role?"

Strategy 3: Justify above market rate
"Given my experience with [specific valuable skill] and 
the impact I'd make, I'd be looking for $X, which is 
[10-20%] above market rate because [specific value prop]."

Never:
❌ Give a single number
❌ Go first if you can avoid it
❌ Lowball yourself
```

### Responding to the Initial Offer

**First: Thank them**:
```
Recruiter: "We'd like to offer you $120,000 base..."

You: "Thank you! I'm excited about the opportunity and 
appreciate you putting this together. Can you send me 
the full details in writing? I'd like to review everything 
and get back to you."

Why:
✓ Shows appreciation
✓ Buys you time to think
✓ Prevents emotional response
✓ Shows you're thoughtful
```

**Never Accept Immediately**:
```
Even if the offer is amazing:

Wrong:
"Yes! I accept! Thank you!"
[Left money on the table]

Right:
"This looks great! I'm very interested. Let me review 
the details and get back to you by [date]. I may have 
a few questions."

Why:
- They expect negotiation
- Immediate acceptance suggests you'd take less
- You might find issues upon review
- You need to negotiate other offers if any

Always take at least 24 hours to review.
```

### Making Your Counter-Offer

**The Formula**:
```
Components of good counter:

1. Express enthusiasm:
   "I'm very excited about this role and the team..."

2. State your value:
   "Given my [specific experience] and [specific 
   skill] that would let me [specific impact]..."

3. Make specific ask:
   "I was hoping for $X base salary, $Y signing bonus, 
   and $Z in equity."

4. Justify with research:
   "This aligns with market rates for similar roles at 
   comparable companies."

5. Ask for confirmation:
   "Is this something we can work toward?"
```

**Example Counter-Offer Email**:
```
Subject: Re: Offer - Senior Software Engineer

Hi [Recruiter],

Thank you so much for the offer! I'm very excited about 
joining the team and working on [specific project/mission]. 
After our conversations, I'm confident I can make significant 
impact, especially around [specific area where you add value].

I've reviewed the offer details, and while I'm excited about 
the opportunity, I was hoping we could adjust the compensation 
to better reflect the market and my experience:

Current offer:
- Base: $130,000
- Signing bonus: $10,000
- Equity: $80,000 over 4 years

Proposed:
- Base: $145,000
- Signing bonus: $20,000
- Equity: $120,000 over 4 years

This would bring the total compensation more in line with 
market rates for senior engineers with my background in 
[specific valuable skill]. I've seen similar roles at 
comparable companies ranging from $X to $Y according to 
levels.fyi and other sources.

I'm confident that my experience with [specific achievement] 
and ability to [specific value] makes this a fair adjustment.

Is this something we can work toward? I'm happy to discuss 
further.

Best regards,
[Your name]
```

**How Much to Ask For**:
```
General guidelines:

Base salary:
- Junior roles: 5-10% above offer
- Mid-level: 10-15% above offer
- Senior: 15-20% above offer

Equity:
- 20-50% more than offered
- Larger range because harder to value

Signing bonus:
- $10k-$50k depending on level
- Can offset first-year equity vesting cliff

Total comp:
- Aim for 10-20% above initial offer

If you have competing offers:
- Can push higher (20-30%+)
- Must be willing to walk away
```

### Negotiation Tactics

**The Competing Offer**:
```
If you have another offer:

"I'm very interested in [Company], but I do have another 
offer from [Other Company] at $X total compensation. 
[Company] is my first choice because [reasons], and I'd 
love to make this work. Can we discuss bridging this gap?"

Power move (risky):
"I have offers from [A], [B], and [C]. You're my top 
choice because [reasons]. Here's what it would take for 
me to accept: [your number]. Otherwise, I'll likely go 
with [A]."

Only do this if:
✓ You actually have other offers
✓ You're willing to walk away
✓ Numbers are specific and provable

Never:
❌ Lie about other offers (unethical + may be called out)
❌ Be vague ("I have other opportunities...")
❌ Use it as only leverage
```

**The Performance Lever**:
```
"I understand the budget constraints. Would it be possible 
to structure this with:
- Starting at $X base
- Performance review at 6 months (instead of 12)
- Adjustment to $Y if I hit these metrics: [specific goals]

This gives the company protection and gives me upside for 
delivering value quickly."

This shows:
✓ Understanding of business
✓ Confidence in your abilities
✓ Flexibility
✓ Focus on value, not just money
```

**The Non-Monetary Levers**:
```
If salary is truly fixed:

Negotiate for:
✓ Signing bonus (one-time, doesn't affect budget)
✓ More equity (diluted value, easier to give)
✓ Earlier performance review (6 months vs 12)
✓ Higher title (affects future compensation)
✓ Remote work flexibility
✓ Extra PTO (vacation days)
✓ Learning budget ($5-10k/year)
✓ Conference attendance
✓ Home office stipend
✓ Relocation assistance

Example:
"If the base salary can't move, could we increase the 
signing bonus to $X and add 2 extra weeks of PTO? That 
would make this work for me."
```

### Handling Push-Back

**"This is our final offer"**:
```
Response:
"I understand. This is a great opportunity and I'm very 
interested. Just to be clear—there's no flexibility on 
any component? Not base, signing bonus, equity, or title?"

Often they'll budge on something.

If truly final:
"I appreciate your candor. Let me think about this and 
get back to you by [date]."

Then:
- Evaluate if it meets your minimum
- Consider non-monetary value
- Decide if you can accept or must decline
```

**"That's above our budget"**:
```
Response:
"I understand budget constraints. What would work within 
your budget? I'm flexible on the structure—we could 
adjust the mix of base, bonus, and equity."

Or:
"That makes sense. What's the top of your range? If it's 
close to $X, I think we can make it work."

Or:
"I appreciate that. Can we revisit this in my first 
performance review if I demonstrate the value I'm 
describing?"

Show flexibility while maintaining your target.
```

**"We need an answer today"**:
```
Push back (politely):
"I want to make a thoughtful decision about something 
this important. Could I have until [tomorrow/2 days] to 
review everything? I promise to get back to you by then."

If they truly won't budge:
"I understand the urgency. Let me review everything 
today and get back to you by end of day."

Never:
❌ Accept pressure to decide immediately
❌ Accept an offer you're not sure about

Rushing you is a red flag—good companies give time.
```

### Closing the Deal

**When You're Happy**:
```
"Thank you so much for working with me on this. The 
revised offer of $X base, $Y equity, and $Z signing 
bonus works great. I'm excited to accept and join the 
team!

Before I sign, could you confirm:
- Start date: [date]
- Title: [exact title]
- Reporting to: [manager name]
- [Any other critical terms]

Once confirmed, I'll sign and return the offer letter 
today."

Get everything in writing before you:
✓ Resign from current job
✓ Reject other offers
✓ Make big purchases based on new income
```

**Declining Gracefully**:
```
If you decide not to accept:

"Thank you so much for the offer and for working with me 
throughout this process. After careful consideration, I've 
decided to pursue another opportunity that's a better fit 
for me at this time.

I've really enjoyed getting to know the team, and I hope 
we can stay in touch for the future.

Best regards,
[Your name]"

Why graceful declination matters:
✓ Tech industry is small
✓ You might want to apply again
✓ Recruiter might move to another company
✓ Professional reputation matters

Never:
❌ Ghost them
❌ Be rude or antagonistic
❌ Over-explain your reasons
```

## Cultural Considerations

### For Non-Western Candidates

**Cultural Differences**:
```
Many cultures view negotiation as:
- Impolite or aggressive
- Showing greed
- Disrespecting the employer
- Risking the offer

Western business culture views negotiation as:
- Normal and expected
- Advocating for yourself
- Business transaction
- Sign of professionalism

Reframe: It's not personal, it's business.
```

**Overcoming Hesitation**:
```
Remember:
✓ Companies budget for negotiation
✓ They won't rescind offer for negotiating
✓ They respect candidates who negotiate
✓ Not negotiating hurts your lifetime earnings
✓ You're advocating for your family's future

Think of it as:
- Professional discussion, not personal
- Standard business practice
- Respectful exchange
- Expected part of process

You can be polite AND firm.
```

### For Women and Underrepresented Groups

**The Penalty**:
```
Research shows:
- Women negotiate less often than men
- When women negotiate, they face more pushback
- Women receive lower initial offers
- Underrepresented groups face similar patterns

Result: Significant lifetime earnings gap
```

**Strategies**:
```
Frame negotiation as:
✓ "We" language: "What can we do to make this work?"
✓ Collaborative: "I'm excited to find a solution..."
✓ Data-driven: "Market research shows..."
✓ Value-focused: "Given the impact I'll make..."

Rather than:
✗ "I want"
✗ "I demand"
✗ Pure self-interest

This frames negotiation as problem-solving together.

That said: You have every right to advocate for yourself 
directly. Use what feels authentic to you.
```

**Push Through Discomfort**:
```
If negotiating feels scary:
1. Practice with friend 5-10 times
2. Write script and rehearse
3. Remember: This is expected
4. Focus on the $100k+ you'll gain
5. Think of it as business, not personal

The discomfort of 1 conversation is worth hundreds of 
thousands of dollars.
```

**The Leverage**:
```
Having multiple offers gives you:
✓ Negotiating power
✓ Confidence to walk away
✓ Comparison data
✓ Time pressure for companies

How to use:
"I'm very excited about [Your Company], but I do have 
competing offers at $X and $Y. You're my first choice 
because [specific reasons]. Is there room to match or 
exceed [better offer]?"

This works because:
- Shows you're in demand
- Gives them specific number to beat
- Shows them as your preference (flattering)

```
If offers have different timelines:

Many companies will:
✓ Speed up their process

Don't:
❌ Accept an offer just because it expires first
❌ Lie about deadlines
❌ Burn bridges by stringing companies along

## Equity Deep Dive

### RSUs vs Options

**Restricted Stock Units (RSUs)**:
```
How they work:
- You receive actual company stock
- Vests over time (typically 4 years)
- Becomes yours when it vests
- Taxed as income when vested
- Can sell immediately if public company

Example:
$100,000 RSUs over 4 years
- Year 1: $25,000 vests → taxed → you own stock
- Year 2: $25,000 vests → taxed → you own stock
- Etc.

Value: $100,000 if stock price stays same
Could be more or less if price changes

Best for: Public companies or late-stage startups
```

**Stock Options**:
```
How they work:
- Right to BUY stock at set price (strike price)
- Vests over time
- Only valuable if company value increases
- Must exercise (buy) to own stock
- Illiquid until IPO or acquisition

Example:
10,000 options at $1 strike price
Company later worth $10/share
- You can buy 10,000 shares for $10,000
- Worth $100,000
- Profit: $90,000 (minus taxes)

But if company worth < $1/share:
- Options worthless ("underwater")

Best for: Early-stage startups with high upside
```

### Evaluating Equity Value

**For Public Companies**:
```
Easy to value:
- Look up current stock price
- Multiply by shares
- That's current value
- Check historical price trends

Example:
100 RSUs, stock at $150/share
Current value: $15,000/year over 4 years

Risk: Stock price could drop
```

**For Private Companies**:
```
Harder to value. Consider:

1. Latest valuation: $1B company
2. Your shares: 10,000
3. Total shares outstanding: 100M
4. Your ownership: 10,000 / 100M = 0.01%
5. Theoretical value: $1B * 0.01% = $100k

But discount for:
- Illiquidity (can't sell yet)
- Dilution (more shares issued)
- Exit probability (might never IPO)
- Liquidation preference (investors paid first)

Realistic value: Maybe 20-50% of theoretical
So $100k theoretical → $20-50k real value

Early stage: Discount even more (10-30%)
Late stage: Less discount (50-70%)
```

**Questions to Ask About Equity**:
```
For private companies:

✓ "What's the current valuation?"
✓ "How many shares outstanding?"
✓ "What's my strike price?" (options)
✓ "What's the liquidation preference?"
✓ "What are the vesting terms?"
✓ "Can I early exercise?" (options)
✓ "What happens to equity if I leave?"
✓ "What's the exit timeline?"
✓ "When was the last funding round?"

Don't be shy—these are standard questions!
```

## After Accepting

### Resigning Professionally

**Notice Period**:
```
Standard: 2 weeks notice
Senior role: 3-4 weeks
Critical role: Offer to train replacement

Never:
❌ Quit immediately
❌ Quit without formal resignation
❌ Bad-mouth company on way out
```

**Resignation Letter**:
```
Dear [Manager],

I'm writing to formally resign from my position as [Title], 
effective [Date, 2+ weeks from now].

I've accepted an opportunity that aligns with my long-term 
career goals. I've greatly appreciated my time here, 
particularly [specific positive experience].

I'm committed to ensuring a smooth transition. I'll document 
my work, train my replacement, and wrap up my ongoing 
projects.

Thank you for the opportunity to work with this team.

Best regards,
[Your name]
```

**Counter-Offers**:
```
If current employer counter-offers:

Consider carefully:
✓ Why did it take you leaving to get this?
✓ Will they remember you "threatened to leave"?
✓ Does it address WHY you wanted to leave?
✓ Is it just buying time to replace you?

Usually better to:
- Thank them
- Stay firm on decision
- Leave professionally

Exception: If ONLY reason was money and they 
match, might be worth staying.
```
